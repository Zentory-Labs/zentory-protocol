The ZENT token (ERC-20 on HyperEVM testnet) serves as the protocol's utility and governance medium, enabling staking, subscription payments, and on-chain governance participation.

The analysis focuses on three primary jurisdictions: the United States, the European Union, and the United Kingdom. These jurisdictions were selected based on the anticipated geographic distribution of ZENT users and the stringency of their respective regulatory frameworks for financial signal provision and crypto-asset services.

The primary regulatory exposures identified are:

- **United States:** Securities law compliance (Howey Test analysis), SEC Investment Advisers Act registration, CFTC Commodity Trading Advisor requirements, and Bank Secrecy Act / FinCEN money services business obligations.
- **European Union:** MiCA fitness determination, MiFID II investment advice registration, and cross-border service restrictions.

ZENT should also retain qualified securities counsel to render a formal Howey Test opinion on the ZENT token before any public token sale, listing, or marketing to U.S. persons. The staking and governance architecture should be reviewed to minimize the risk that ZENT is deemed an unregistered security under U.S. law.

This memo is a research document prepared for internal discussion purposes only. It does not constitute legal advice. ## 2. United States Regulatory Framework

### 2.A. Is ZENT a Security? — Howey Test Analysis

The determination of whether a crypto asset constitutes a "security" under U.S. law is governed primarily by the Howey Test, derived from *SEC v. W.J. Howey Co.* (1946). Under this framework, an investment of money in a common enterprise with a reasonable expectation of profits to be derived from the entrepreneurial or managerial efforts of others constitutes a security. The SEC has applied this test to numerous token sales and has brought enforcement actions where tokens were sold as securities without registration or applicable exemption.

The application of the Howey Test to the ZENT token yields the following analysis:

**Investment of Money — YES.** ZENT tokens are purchased by holders. The purchase of ZENT— whether on a secondary market, through a token generation event, or via subscription payment denominated in ZENT— constitutes an investment of money. This prong is clearly satisfied.

**Common Enterprise — YES.** ZENT token holders participate in a common enterprise. Staking rewards are funded from two primary sources: (i) performance fees generated by signal providers whose strategies are facilitated through the protocol, and (ii) subscription fees paid by subscribers for signal access. Both revenue streams are pooled through protocol mechanics and distributed to ZENT stakers on a pro-rata basis. This pooling of economic interest among token holders satisfies the common enterprise prong.

**Expectation of Profit — YES.** ZENT holders acquire tokens with a reasonable expectation of profit. This expectation arises from multiple vectors: (a) staking rewards (yield) distributed in ZENT or in protocol revenue, (b) potential appreciation in the market price of ZENT driven by protocol growth and token demand, and (c) governance token value accrual as the protocol captures additional signal market share. These profit expectations are explicitly articulated in ZENT's marketing materials and are a principal driver of token acquisition demand.

**From the Efforts of Others — CONTESTED.** This is the most heavily litigated prong in recent crypto securities cases. The SEC has increasingly argued that the efforts of a decentralized protocol's founders, development team, or affiliated entities satisfy this prong, particularly where: (i) the team continues to develop and maintain the protocol, (ii) the token's value is driven by team efforts rather than purely market forces, or (iii) the token's economic model relies on active management. ZENT's position has some force in that: (a) staking rewards are algorithmically determined by protocol rules rather than discretionary distributions, and (b) ZENT holders participate in governance, creating a degree of decentralization. However, the argument weakens considerably if: (i) a ZENT Foundation or team retains significant token allocations, (ii) the team actively promotes token investment, or (iii) the protocol's growth is driven primarily by team-led business development rather than purely by open-source participation.

**Key Regulatory Risk:** If ZENT is deemed a security, every offer and sale of ZENT to U.S. persons must either be registered with the SEC or qualify for an exemption. The most likely applicable exemptions for a token sale are Regulation D (Rule 506(c) — accredited investors only) or Regulation S (offshore sales only). If ZENT is sold to non-accredited U.S. investors without registration or a valid exemption, ZENT Protocol LLC and its founders face potential civil and criminal liability under Section 5 of the Securities Act of 1933.

**Recommendation:** Obtain a formal written legal opinion from qualified securities counsel regarding the ZENT token's status under the Howey Test. Until such opinion is obtained, avoid any public marketing or sale of ZENT to U.S. persons. Consider restructuring the ZENT tokenomics to reduce Howey Test exposure: this may include (i) eliminating or reducing staking yield, (ii) ensuring that governance rewards are nominal and non-investment-motivated, and (iii) accelerating decentralization of protocol governance to reduce the "efforts of others" argument.

### 2.B. Signal Provision — Investment Adviser vs. Broker-Dealer

Providing trading signals for financial instruments may trigger registration requirements under U.S. securities law. The applicable regulatory framework depends on the nature of the signals, the compensation structure, and whether the signals relate to securities or non-securities.

#### Investment Adviser Registration (SEC)

Under the Investment Advisers Act of 1940, any person who, for compensation, engages in the business of advising others about the value of or investing in securities must register with the SEC as an investment adviser ("RIA"), unless an exemption applies.

**Application to ZENT:** If ZENT's signal provision is deemed to constitute "investment advice" with respect to securities, ZENT must register as an RIA. The SEC has historically interpreted "investment advice" broadly. AI-generated trading signals that recommend specific buy or sell actions for securities could reasonably be characterized as investment advice. The fact that ZENT does not charge a fee directly tied to signal performance does not necessarily insulate it from RIA registration; the receipt of staking rewards and subscription fees in ZENT tokens could be deemed compensation for advisory services.

**RIA Registration Requirements:**

- **Form ADV:** Initial registration with the SEC via Form ADV, disclosing AUM, number of clients, compensation arrangements, and disciplinary history. Annual amendments required.
- **Compliance Program:** Written policies and procedures addressing conflicts of interest, custody, and trading practices.
- **Custody Rules:** If ZENT holds client funds or securities, custody rules under Rule 206(4)-2 apply.
- **Performance-Based Compensation:** Section 203(b)(3) of the Investment Advisers Act provides an exemption from RIA registration for investment advisers with fewer than 15 clients and who do not hold themselves out generally as investment advisers. However, the performance-based compensation prohibition under Section 205 generally prohibits RIA agreements that are compensated based on capital gains or capital appreciation. If ZENT staking rewards are structured as a share of trading profits, this could trigger Section 205 issues. However, if rewards are derived from subscription fees and are not tied to capital gains, the exemption analysis may differ.

**Practical Recommendation:** The RIA registration exemption under Section 203(b)(3) is worth analyzing carefully for ZENT if it is providing securities signals. If ZENT limits its U.S. securities signal business to fewer than 15 clients who are all qualified purchasers or accredited investors, and does not hold itself out generally as an investment adviser, it may be able to operate without SEC registration in the near term. However, this is a fact-intensive analysis that requires formal legal review.

**Testing the Waters:** Under Regulation D, Rule 241, issuers can conduct "testing the waters" activities with potential accredited investors prior to a registered offering. If ZENT conducts a token sale under Rule 506(c), it may legally solicit and advertise to accredited investors only, prior to the sale. This requires robust accredited investor verification procedures.

#### Broker-Dealer Registration (FINRA)

Broker-dealer registration with FINRA is required if ZENT, in connection with the sale of securities: (i) receives transaction-based compensation (commissions, spreads, markups), or (ii) effects transactions in securities.

**Application to ZENT:** If ZENT charges transaction-based fees tied to the execution of trades (rather than flat subscription fees), broker-dealer registration may be required. If ZENT receives a spread or revenue share from affiliated or third-party exchanges where subscribers trade, this could constitute transaction-based compensation.

**Broker-Dealer Registration Requirements:**

- **Form BD:** Initial registration with FINRA and the SEC.
- **Net Capital Rule (SEC Rule 15c3-1):** Minimum net capital requirements based on the nature of business.
- **SIPC Membership:** Securities Investor Protection Corporation membership is required for broker-dealers handling customer securities.
- **Extensive Compliance Infrastructure:** Written supervisory procedures, anti-money laundering programs, customer information security programs, and annual financial reporting.

**Exemption Analysis:** If ZENT's signals relate exclusively to crypto assets that are not securities, broker-dealer registration is almost certainly not required under current U.S. law, as FINRA has taken the position that it does not have jurisdiction over pure crypto activities (the securities/commodities distinction is determinative). However, if ZENT provides signals for any security tokens, equities, ETFs, or other registered securities, the broker-dealer question must be re-analyzed.

**Practical Recommendation:** ZENT should implement geo-blocking for equity securities signals to U.S. users until the investment adviser and broker-dealer questions are resolved by qualified counsel. Crypto-only signals (BTC, ETH, other non-security crypto assets) present substantially lower regulatory risk under current SEC/FINRA frameworks.

### 2.C. CFTC Jurisdiction (Commodities)

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission ("CFTC") has jurisdiction over commodity derivatives markets and, following the 2021 infrastructure bill and subsequent CFTC guidance, broad enforcement authority over spot crypto assets that are deemed commodities, including Bitcoin (BTC) and Ethereum (ETH).

**Application to ZENT:**

- **Crypto Signals:** For BTC, ETH, and other CFTC-regulated commodities, CFTC has jurisdiction. Providing trading signals for these commodities does not automatically trigger CTA registration, but if ZENT's activities cross certain thresholds, registration may be required.
- **Forex Signals:** The forex market is not a regulated exchange market for retail forex in the same manner as futures. The CFTC has jurisdiction over forex swaps and forwards, but retail forex trading by U.S. persons is primarily regulated by the NFA (National Futures Association) for registered futures merchants. If ZENT provides forex signals to U.S. retail traders, CFTC CTA and NFA registration may be implicated.
- **Commodity Futures Signals:** If ZENT provides signals for regulated commodity futures (energy, agricultural, metals), CTA registration is more likely required.

**Commodity Trading Advisor (CTA) Registration:**

Under the Commodity Exchange Act, any person who, for compensation, advises others about the value of or investing in commodity futures or forex must register as a CTA, unless an exemption applies.

**CTA Exemption — "Solely Incidental" Test:** A CTA is not required to register if its advice is "solely incidental" to its principal business. For ZENT, if the provision of commodity or forex signals is not the primary business purpose, this exemption may be available. However, the CFTC and NFA have interpreted this narrowly. If ZENT's primary business is signal provision (and not, for example, a registered futures commission merchant or commodity broker), the exemption is unlikely to apply.

**Recommendation:** Consult CFTC and NFA counsel before offering forex or commodity futures signals to U.S. persons. At a minimum, implement geo-blocking for forex signals pending legal clarity.

### 2.D. Anti-Money Laundering (AML/BSA)

The Bank Secrecy Act ("BSA") imposes AML obligations on "money services businesses" ("MSBs"). An MSB is defined to include persons who issue, sell, or redeem money orders, traveler's checks, or other similar instruments, or who engage in money transmission. An MSB must:

- Register with FinCEN (the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network) if it conducts more than $1,000 in money transmission per person per year.
- Implement a written AML compliance program.
- File Suspicious Activity Reports ("SARs") for transactions that involve $2,000 or more and evidence knowledge or suspicion of money laundering.
- File Currency Transaction Reports ("CTRs") for cash transactions exceeding $10,000.

**Application to ZENT:** ZENT's subscription payment processing — whether denominated in fiat (via Stripe) or in ZENT tokens — could constitute money transmission if ZENT is processing payments between subscribers and signal providers. Specifically:

- If ZENT collects subscription fees in fiat or crypto and distributes a portion to signal providers, this could constitute money transmission.
- If ZENT converts subscription payments from one currency to another (e.g., USD to ZENT), money transmission is clearly implicated.

**FinCEN MSB Registration:** If ZENT processes more than $1,000 per person per year in subscription payments, FinCEN MSB registration is required. This is a low bar for any functioning subscription business.

**KYC Implementation:** ZENT should implement know-your-customer ("KYC") procedures for all subscription payment processing. At minimum, this should include collection of name, email address, and for transactions exceeding $1,000 per person per year, government-issued identification. Stripe's own KYC/AML tools may satisfy a portion of this obligation for fiat transactions, but ZENT's use of Stripe's platform does not automatically satisfy ZENT's independent BSA obligations.

**Travel Rule:** For transfers of ZENT tokens that constitute money transmission (e.g., ZENT transfers between wallets facilitated by the protocol), FATF "Travel Rule" compliance may be required. The Travel Rule requires financial institutions to collect, transmit, and receive originator and beneficiary information for transfers above a threshold (typically $3,000). ZENT should implement Travel Rule compliance tooling before facilitating any token transfers that exceed applicable thresholds.

## 3. European Union Regulatory Framework

### 3.A. MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation)

MiCA entered into force in 2023, with full application from December 30, 2024. It establishes a comprehensive regulatory framework for crypto-asset issuance, custody, exchange, and advisory services across the EU.

**Scope of MiCA:** MiCA applies to "crypto-assets," defined as digital representations of value or rights that can be transferred and stored electronically, using distributed ledger technology or similar. Critically, MiCA explicitly excludes:

- Crypto-assets that qualify as "financial instruments" under MiFID II (these are regulated under existing EU securities law).
- Utility tokens that are intended to provide access to a service or application that is not a financial instrument.

**ZENT Token Classification under MiCA:**

The threshold question is whether ZENT is a "utility token," an "investment token," or a "crypto-asset" subject to MiCA. Under ESMA guidance:

- **Utility Token:** If ZENT's primary purpose is to provide access to ZENT Protocol's signal exchange services (i.e., ZENT must be staked or held to access services), ZENT qualifies as a utility token and falls outside MiCA's scope.
- **Investment Token:** If ZENT is marketed as a security-like instrument with profit expectations (staking rewards, governance rights with economic value), ZENT may be classified as an investment token, triggering MiCA's requirement for a Crypto-Asset Whitepaper ("CAW") and potential CASPs registration.

**Signal Provision under MiCA:** MiCA does not regulate the provision of trading signals per se. MiCA's regulatory scope covers token issuance, crypto-asset custody, exchange services, and certain advisory activities. If ZENT does not issue a new token (ZENT is already issued), does not operate as a crypto-asset exchange, and does not provide investment advice within MiCA's scope, MiCA compliance obligations are limited.

**Recommendation:** MiCA compliance is a lower priority for ZENT at its current stage compared to U.S. regulatory requirements. ZENT should, however, monitor ESMA's ongoing guidance on utility token classification, as the line between utility and investment tokens remains fact-specific and subject to evolving interpretation. Before any marketing of ZENT to EU persons, a formal MiCA classification opinion should be obtained.

### 3.B. MiFID II — Investment Services

The Markets in Financial Instruments Directive II ("MiFID II") and the accompanying MiFIR regulation establish the framework for investment services and activities within the EU. If ZENT's signal provision constitutes "investment advice" under MiFID II, ZENT would need to be registered as an investment firm in an EU member state.

**Investment Advice under MiFID II:** MiFID II defines investment advice as the provision of personalized recommendations to a client regarding a transaction in financial instruments. A recommendation is "personalized" if it is issued to a person in their capacity as an investor or potential investor and if it relates to the merits of a specific transaction or type of transaction. AI-generated signals that are customized or subscriber-specific could constitute investment advice under this definition.

**Registration Requirements if MiFID II Applies:**

- Authorization as an investment firm in an EU member state (e.g., through a national competent authority such as Germany's BaFin or France's AMF).
- Initial capital requirement: between €50,000 and €730,000 depending on the firm's investment activities and AUM.
- Ongoing compliance with MiFID II's organizational requirements, best execution obligations, and product governance rules.
- Passporting rights to serve clients across EU member states once authorized in one jurisdiction.

**Cross-Border Service Restrictions:** EU passporting rules permit investment firms authorized in one member state to provide services across the EU without additional authorization in each member state. However, if ZENT is not authorized in any EU member state (i.e., it is a U.S.-based entity), it cannot passport services into the EU. Cross-border provision of investment advice to EU retail clients by an unauthorized foreign firm is prohibited.

**Recommendation:** ZENT should not market equity, forex, or regulated commodity signals to EU persons until MiFID II compliance status is clarified by qualified EU regulatory counsel. Implement geo-blocking for EU users for all signals other than crypto spot and perpetuals (which are not "financial instruments" under MiFID II, as they are not yet classified as such under EU law) pending legal review.

### 3.C. Cross-Border Services and Reverse Solicitation

Even where an entity is not authorized in the EU, it may receive clients through the "reverse solicitation" exemption. Under this exemption, if an EU client initiates contact with a foreign firm entirely on their own initiative — without any marketing, solicitation, or inducement from the firm — the foreign firm may provide services to that client without EU authorization.

**Application to ZENT:** If EU persons discover ZENT organically (e.g., through non-EU-directed marketing, word of mouth, or organic search), ZENT may argue the reverse solicitation exemption applies. However, this exemption is narrow and does not permit active marketing to EU clients. ZENT's website, marketing materials, and advertising must not be directed at EU persons if ZENT is not EU-authorized.

**Important Note:** ESMA and national competent authorities have been increasingly aggressive in scrutinizing reverse solicitation claims. If ZENT's marketing materials are accessible in EU languages, or if ZENT's business development activities could be construed as targeted at EU clients, the reverse solicitation exemption may not apply.

## 4. United Kingdom Regulatory Framework

### 4.A. FCA Crypto Asset Registration

As of October 2023, the UK Financial Conduct Authority ("FCA") requires all UK-based cryptoasset businesses to register under the Money Laundering Regulations (MLR). This registration is mandatory for any business that conducts cryptoasset activity in the UK, including issuing, storing, transferring, or exchanging crypto assets.

**Application to ZENT:** If ZENT Protocol LLC is incorporated or operating in the UK, FCA registration is mandatory. If ZENT is a non-UK entity (e.g., a U.S. LLC) providing services to UK persons, the analysis is more complex:

- The UK government's "static" vs. "dynamic" approach to jurisdiction determines whether UK AML regulations apply to overseas entities. Under the dynamic approach, if a foreign entity is "carrying on a business" in the UK, UK AML regulations may apply even if the entity has no physical presence in the UK.
- The FCA has been explicit that overseas crypto businesses that market to UK clients may be subject to UK regulations, even if they are registered in another jurisdiction.

**Recommendation:** Determine ZENT Protocol's legal domicile and the physical location of its operations. If any operations are conducted from the UK, FCA registration is mandatory. If ZENT is a US entity with no UK presence, assess whether its marketing activities could be construed as "carrying on business" in the UK. Implement geo-blocking for UK users for all crypto-asset activities if there is any ambiguity.

---

### 4.B. Investment Advice — FCA Conduct of Business (COBS)

The FCA's Conduct of Business Sourcebook ("COBS") requires that any person who, in the course of business, provides investment advice to retail clients must be FCA-authorized (or be an appointed representative of an FCA-authorized firm).

**Application to ZENT:** If ZENT provides trading signals for securities, forex, or regulated crypto assets to UK persons, and those signals are deemed "investment advice" under FCA rules, ZENT must be FCA-authorized as an investment adviser. Unauthorized provision of investment advice to UK retail clients is a criminal offense under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 ("FSMA").

**FCA Authorization Process:**

- **Investment Adviser Authorization:** Applying for FCA authorization as an investment adviser is a complex, multi-month process. The FCA scrutinizes the applicant's business model, governance, financial resources, and compliance infrastructure.
- **Timeline and Cost:** FCA authorization for investment advisers typically takes 12-18 months. Application fees range from £1,500 to £25,000 depending on the type of authorization. Ongoing supervision and compliance costs are substantial.
- **Appointed Representative Route:** As an alternative, ZENT could operate as an "appointed representative" of an FCA-authorized firm, which would allow it to provide investment advice under the authorized firm's supervision. This route requires finding a suitable FCA-authorized firm willing to sponsor ZENT.

**Recommendation:** Implement geo-blocking for UK users for all non-crypto signals (equities, forex, regulated commodities) until FCA compliance is established. Crypto signals present a lower regulatory risk under FCA rules, as the FCA's crypto regulatory framework is still developing, but the FCA has signaled that it intends to regulate crypto asset activities more aggressively.

## 5. Multi-Asset Signal Complications

ZENT's multi-asset signal marketplace introduces a layered regulatory complexity, as each asset class is subject to a distinct regulatory regime. The following analysis grades the regulatory risk of each signal category by jurisdiction.

### Equity Signals — Highest Regulatory Risk

Equity trading signals are the highest-risk signal category for ZENT. Providing buy or sell recommendations on listed securities (stocks, ETFs, ADRs) in exchange for compensation (in any form — subscription fees, staking rewards, token payments) likely triggers:

- **United States:** SEC investment adviser registration (RIA) or exemption; potential FINRA broker-dealer registration if transaction-based; state-level "Blue Sky" registrations; Section 5 liability if ZENT is deemed an unregistered security sold to non-accredited U.S. investors.
- **European Union:** MiFID II investment advice authorization in the relevant member state; CAW and CASP registration under MiCA if ZENT tokens are involved; cross-border service prohibition for non-authorized foreign firms.
- **United Kingdom:** FCA investment adviser authorization requirement; criminal offense under FSMA for unauthorized investment advice.

**Practical Impact:** ZENT should implement immediate geo-blocking for equity signals for all users in the US, EU, and UK. The legal cost and compliance burden of authorizing equity signal provision in these jurisdictions is prohibitive at ZENT's current stage, and the liability risk of operating without authorization is existential.

### Forex Signals — Medium-High Regulatory Risk

Forex signals present significant but somewhat lower regulatory risk than equity signals, primarily because the forex market is less regulated at the retail level.

- **United States:** CFTC jurisdiction over forex as a commodity; CTA registration may be required for forex signal provision; NFA registration may be required if forex is provided in connection with a registered Futures Commission Merchant.
- **European Union:** MiFID II applies to forex as a financial instrument; investment advice authorization required; cross-border service prohibition applies.
- **United Kingdom:** FCA investment advice authorization required for forex signals to UK clients; FCA is actively scrutinizing retail forex and CFD signal providers.

**Practical Impact:** Geo-block forex signals for US, EU, and UK users pending CFTC/FCA counsel opinions. Crypto-settled forex products (e.g., forex pairs settled in stablecoins rather than fiat) may present lower risk, but this area is evolving.

### Commodity Futures Signals — Medium Regulatory Risk

Commodity futures signals (energy, agricultural, metals) are subject to CFTC jurisdiction in the US and FCA jurisdiction in the UK. The CTA registration question is most acute for commodity futures.

- **United States:** CFTC CTA registration likely required if commodity futures signals are provided as a primary business; NFA registration required if dealing with retail forex in connection with commodity trading.
- **European Union:** MiFID II applies to commodity futures as financial instruments; authorization required.
- **United Kingdom:** FCA investment advice authorization required.

**Practical Impact:** Geo-block commodity futures signals for US, EU, and UK users.

### Crypto Signals — Lowest Regulatory Risk

Crypto signals (BTC, ETH, other non-security crypto assets) present the lowest regulatory risk of the signal categories:

- **United States:** CFTC has jurisdiction over crypto as commodities; pure crypto signal provision (without securities exposure) does not require SEC investment adviser registration or FINRA broker-dealer registration under current regulatory frameworks. CFTC CTA registration may still be required if forex or commodity futures signals are provided.
- **European Union:** MiCA does not regulate signal provision per se; if ZENT is classified as a utility token, MiCA obligations are minimal; MiFID II does not apply to non-security crypto assets.
- **United Kingdom:** FCA crypto registration required for UK-based businesses; non-UK crypto signal providers operating cross-border may be subject to UK AML rules but face lower barriers than equity/forex signal providers.

**Practical Impact:** Crypto signals (spot and perpetuals) can be offered in the US, EU, and UK, subject to standard AML/KYC requirements and jurisdiction-specific registration if ZENT has a physical presence in those jurisdictions.

## 7. Red Flags That Trigger Regulatory Attention

The following marketing practices, business conduct patterns, and product features have historically attracted regulatory scrutiny and, in some cases, have been the basis for enforcement actions. ZENT should actively avoid each of the following:

1. **Guaranteed Returns:** Promising specific returns, accuracy rates, or profit targets in any marketing materials, product documentation, or communications with potential users. The SEC's marketing rule for investment advisers and the FTC's guidance on deceptive practices both prohibit unsubstantiated performance claims. Never state or imply that ZENT signals will generate profit.

2. **Celebrity and Influencer Endorsements:** SEC rules on testimonial advertising (Investment Advisers Act Rule 206(4)-1) prohibit investment advisers from using testimonials or endorsements in advertising unless specific disclosures are made. In the context of token sales, the SEC has brought enforcement actions against issuers that used celebrity endorsements without adequate disclosure of compensation. Any promotional arrangement with an influencer or celebrity for ZENT marketing must be reviewed by securities counsel before publication.

3. **"Risk-Free" or "Guaranteed Profit" Claims:** All trading involves risk. Any marketing language that claims a strategy is "risk-free," "guaranteed," or "capital protected" is a direct red flag for the SEC, CFTC, and FCA. These claims are per se violations of securities and commodities advertising rules.

4. **Marketing Securities-Related Products to Non-Accredited U.S. Investors:** Under SEC Regulation D, Rule 506(c), general solicitation and advertising of securities offerings to non-accredited investors is prohibited. If ZENT is deemed a security and is offered under Rule 506(c), marketing must be restricted to verified accredited investors only. Failure to restrict marketing in this manner could invalidate the exemption and cause ZENT's token sale to be an unregistered securities offering.

5. **No KYC/AML on Subscription Payments:** Collecting subscription fees or processing ZENT token transfers without implementing identity verification triggers FinCEN MSB obligations and may constitute a BSA violation. Any subscription payment — fiat or crypto — should be processed through a KYC-compliant payment processor (Stripe, Coinbase Commerce, etc.) or through an in-protocol KYC process.

6. **Unregistered Securities Sales to Non-Accredited Investors:** If ZENT is deemed a security under the Howey Test and is sold to non-accredited U.S. investors without SEC registration or an applicable exemption, ZENT Protocol LLC and its founders face potential civil and criminal liability under Section 5 of the Securities Act of 1933. Civil penalties include disgorgement of all profits and interest; criminal penalties include fines up to $5 million and imprisonment up to 20 years.

7. **Undisclosed Conflicts of Interest:** If ZENT token holders or governance participants benefit from signal provider selection, subscription fee pricing, or staking reward allocation in ways not disclosed to users, this constitutes an undisclosed material conflict of interest under investment adviser fiduciary standards.

8. **Smart Contract Admin Keys and Investor Loss:** If ZENT's smart contracts contain upgrade authority or admin keys that allow the ZENT team to modify token supply, staking rules, or subscription terms in ways that materially affect investor value, and these risks are not disclosed, this could constitute securities fraud if ZENT is deemed a security and if such modifications cause investor losses.
